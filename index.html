Part 1: B

Hypothesis: Professor Wirfs-Brock only visited any given destination location two times.

  • To easily and quickly group points close together, I simply rounded the decimal points of the coordinates. I checked how much time was spent in that location. Then I grouped them by distance and time, counting how many different times a distance was recorded.
  • This rough way of grouping points by time and location was not ideal because it didn't allow me much control over fine-tuning the constraints. This resulted in some innacuracies. One example is that the number of points representing the Marcus Whitman Hotel is very sensitive to the amount of time required to be at a location for it to count as a visit. The hotel is represented by three different location points if a minimum time of 20 minutes is used. This means that the visit count was innacurate because subtle innacuracies in the gps measurements were large enough to give different points when rounded. If I were to be more aggressive with the rounding, however, I would have to trade off with things like the distinction between Olin Hall and the Science Hall.
  • Despite the crude calculations, we can still be informed from the resulting information if we keep in mind the limitations of our approach. It can also push us in the right direction for further analysis. For example, we can see that Professor Wirfs-Brock visited the Science Hall and Olin Hall about the same number of times. We can follow this up by looking at the line graph of distance to each location identified by the grouping. With this closer look, we can see that, as usual, reality is more complicated than the initial grouping and counting would suggest.
